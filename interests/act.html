All the world's a stage, and all the men and women merely players: they have their exits and their entrances; and one man in his time plays many parts, his acts being seven ages.
      -William Shakespeare.

That stage comes to life, if the space between the entrance and the exit is filled with some entertainment, excitement and drama. That life will serve a purpose, if one is enlightened in that process.

I acted in several plays. Some of them are here:
  • as a henpecked husband in When X > Y
    a drama exploring the world in which the role played by the gender is reversed ( I enter @ 4.00 mins)

  • as a narrator in Design for Secureing Sita
    a short fantacy skit exploring contemporary Ramayana

  • as a dancer in How not to Dance
    a fusion of Bharat Natyam and some popular Bollywood steps. I always wanted to perform classical dance on stage but I never learnt classical dance. I fulfilled my wish this way. I learnt how difficult it is to master these art forms

But all of them were cast in an informal set-up. You act with your instincts and by impersonating the characters's closest match in real-world. But what is acting? Is there a scientific approach to acting. I got a chance to learn about acting in a professional set-up when Prof. Micheal Greenwald of the Dept. of Performance Studies at TAMU was screening Indian actors for his adaptation of Susan Townsend The Great Celestial cow

    I played as a cameraman as well as a young groom and these characters' histrionics were to bring a comic relief to an otherwise series play. To my surprise, Prof. Greenwald did not direct us per say but instead he corrected and suggested adjustments to our acting, which helped all of us (the ametuer lot) to be at ease and present our best. Just to satify my curiosity, I borrowed videos on method acting introduced by Stanislavski from the library and to some extent benifited from this experience.This is what the local newspapers have to say about our play:

      Holy Cow (The Battalion)

      Indian actors lend authenticity to play (The Eagle)

    For the first time in my life, I gave my autograph to a fellow Aggie. That was an incredible feeling.

A keen sense of observation, and the observation of the self and the surroundings, play greater role in bridging the gap between "acting" and "living". After all, world is a stage and we are mere players.
